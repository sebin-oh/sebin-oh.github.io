Central Limit Theorem and Maximum Entropy Principle

Central Limit Theorem (CLT) says, if you aggregate a bunch of (nearly independent) randomness, you'll see a Gaussian distribution.

Meanwhile, the Principle of Maximum Entropy says, if the mean and covariance (i.e., statistical information up to second-order central moments) are the only information you have, the most reasonable distribution (least-biased distribution) describing your problem is a Gaussian distribution.

→ The information about your nearly independent randomness that is meaningful and will be preserved at a global perspective is the mean and covariance. Every other detailed information, including higher-order moments, will be washed out at a high-level perspective.



Positive Semi-Definiteness

I used to think about the positive semi-definiteness (PSD) of matrices in terms of the convexity of the problem associated with the matrix, but that’s not a concise or complete understanding.

Positive semi-definiteness is essentially a notion of "positiveness" for matrices.

Consider a general square matrix A.
We can decompose A into symmetric and skew-symmetric parts: A = Asym + Aasym (and if A is symmetric, then A = Asym).
The symmetric part Asym defines a quadratic form. That is, the expression f(x) = xTAsymx represents a paraboloid.
If xTAsymx ≥ 0 for all x, it means the paraboloid lies entirely on or above the zero level.

Note that the Hessian of f(x) is 2Asym, i.e., ∇²f(x) = 2Asym.

That being said, PSD is a sufficient—and, under symmetry, also a necessary—condition for the convexity of a quadratic form:
    A is PSD → xTA x is convex
    xTA x is convex → A is PSD (if A is symmetric)
