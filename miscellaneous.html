Central Limit Theorem and Maximum Entropy Principle

Central Limit Theorem (CLT) says, if you aggregate a bunch of (nearly independent) randomness, you'll see a Gaussian distribution.

Meanwhile, the Principle of Maximum Entropy says, if the mean and covariance (i.e., statistical information up to second-order central moments) are the only information you have, the most reasonable distribution (least-biased distribution) describing your problem is a Gaussian distribution.

→ The information about your nearly independent randomness that is meaningful and will be preserved at a global perspective is the mean and covariance. Every other detailed information, including higher-order moments, will be washed out at a high-level perspective.



Positive Semi-Definiteness

I used to think about the positive semi-definiteness (PSD) of matrices in terms of the convexity of the problem associated with the matrix, but that’s not a concise understanding.
Positive semi-definiteness is essentially a notion of "positiveness" for matrices.
Consider a general square matrix \( A \). We can decompose \( A \) into symmetric and skew-symmetric parts: \( A = A_{\text{sym}} + A_{\text{asym}} \) (and if \( A \) is symmetric, then \( A = A_{\text{sym}} \)). The symmetric part \( A_{\text{sym}} \) defines a quadratic form. That is, the expression \( f(x) = x^\top A_{\text{sym}} x \) represents a paraboloid. If \( x^\top A_{\text{sym}} x \geq 0 \) for all \( x \), it means the paraboloid lies entirely on or above the zero level, making the paraboloid essentially convex.
Note that the Hessian of \( f(x) \) is \( \nabla^2 f(x) = 2A_{\text{sym}} \).
That being said, PSD is a sufficient—and, under symmetry, also a necessary—condition for the convexity of a quadratic form:     \( A \text{ is PSD} \Rightarrow x^\top A x \text{ is convex} \)
    \( x^\top A x \text{ is convex} \Rightarrow A \text{ is PSD (if } A \text{ is symmetric)} \)
